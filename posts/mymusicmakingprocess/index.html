Part one: Inside my music creating process

My first writeup of a two part article series detailing creative tendencies I have and how to get started
February 9, 2022
Reading Time: 4 mins

To be honest with you, I find making music rather difficult sometimes.

I’ll spend weeks on end writing and recording, only to finally demise in a state of endless self pity as I then proceed to listen to songs of Tom Misch and Leon Bridges on repeat, finding myself wondering:

How on Earth am I going to produce one decent song?

As draining as this whole creation process may sound, I can say it is one of the utmost satisfying feelings you can have. To know you’ve put in the effort, emotion and energy in delivering a song to the open world is a rewarding and unparalleled feeling.

So amigos, let’s try to move past the unspoken hurdle of creating 50 different projects, 100s of samples and still not winding up with a song you are happy.

Welcome to my world of creative processes that I use to make my music “unique”.

Be cool to see how this one ages hehe…

Firstly, keep it simple stupid#

I find that my best musical ideas often arise from the simplest of origins. Ironically, it is also my worst ideas (or rather the downfalls of my good ideas) that sprout from trying to incorporate too much into my song.

This is why I generally try to adhere to a blanket rule: KISS

For those who’ve never encountered this acronym before (which is actually remarkably useful for a wide variety of scenarios), it stands for keep it simple stupid. Approaching my music with this mindset helps me to develop my sound whilst producing songs that have a particular layer providing the “oomph” behind everything else in the song.

I love messing around with chords on synths and fingerpicking rhythms on a guitar. More often than not, I try to use these niche interests to my advantage by first coming up with a harmony built upon these sounds and will then proceed to lay a basic beat underneath it.

Usually after doing this, I have this kind of sense of whether I want to pursue the song or leave it on the backburner for the time being.

But then again, where is the fun in creating music if you straight up copy someone else! Drawing inspiration from this, try experimenting with whatever sounds you enjoy in a primitive arrangment (a good guideline is limit to 2-3 layers).

Use weird sounds#

Raindrops. Cars taking off. iPhone notifications. Bubbles. Birds chirping. Friends screaming. Friends crying. Vinyl crackles.

Honestly, go for it and use them all!

Ok, maybe not all of them on a single track (we don’t want to ruin people’s ears) but using a few unusual sounds that are otherwise not “expected” to be in music is actually a great way to make things interesting. I usually love to use them as I approach a transition or am in the second verse as it gives character to the music. Even by simply whipping out your phone and recording the sounds of your surroundings, you can come up with a cool creative sound to engineer into your songs. Not only do they often help complete a song, but they also serve as an interesting story to share with friends something along the lines of:

“Remember when I recorded Bobby’s whole wet ass fart? Well I put it in my latest song lol”

You get the idea. I also love to sample weird sounds and sometimes, they can even form a foundational layer when I’m building a track by the earlier KISS principle.

Give these a go next time you are away at the keys and see just how much of a difference it can make!

Retro 80s-00s style synths#

This is something very special to me which I love to use in music (ooo and maybe on new tracks). My dad used to play heaps of artists along the lines of Daft Punk, Calvin Harris and Sneaky Sound System when I was younger.

Something about the buzz of an analog synth that you can hear used often by these artists just resonated with me in such an exciting way that when I finally came around to creating music around 12, I just couldn’t resist these beautiful sounds.

What we can all learn from this is that finding sounds which you like can originate from a passionate and emotional place. In the same way that I became addicted to using retro style analog synths in music, I encourage you to take a look into what you believe inspires your music and incorporate that into your songs!

And that’s a wrap (sort of)#

So now that you’re probably etching away to get back to your laptop and make a killer track, don’t forget to check back in for the second half of this article where I’ll be going into a bit more depth about more creative decisions I make, my experiences using a DAW and how to get your music out there!
